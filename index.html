SteerDuplex

Steerable Duplex Speech Dialogue Models

Abstract

Full-duplex spoken dialogue models are increasingly competent at low-latency turn-taking, interruption handling, and backchanneling, yet a key capability remains underexplored: steerability—the ability to reliably shift conversational behavior along target attributes such as tone, persona, speaking speed, voice style, and mid-conversation edits in response to user instructions. We present the first systematic empirical analysis of steerability in current full-duplex models and find that they fall substantially short on both text-based and audio-based steering despite their strong interactive behavior.

To close this gap we introduce SteerDuplex, a reinforcement-learning post-training method that couples Group Relative Policy Optimization with a composite reward mixing (i) audio-aware rubric scores judged jointly over the generated speech and its transcript with (ii) programmatically verifiable signals on objectively measurable attributes. Alongside the method we release SteerBench, a hand-graded benchmark of 390 spoken prompts and 1,067 binary audio and text rubrics covering tone, persona, style/accent, and speed/length.

Key Innovation: A composite, multiplicatively-gated reward that combines audio-aware LLM rubrics with verifiable signals and audio-health gates—so that a severe failure in steerability, safety, timing, or audio quality cannot be hidden by a strong score elsewhere. No isolated reward family suffices; the composition is what produces stable steerability gains without breaking the streaming interface.

Main Contributions

📏 SteerBench

The first benchmark for steerability in full-duplex spoken dialogue: 390 prompts and 1,067 hand-written rubrics grading both content and delivery against per-prompt reference clips across tone, persona, style/accent, and speed/length.

🎯 SteerDuplex Recipe

A small-step, KL-constrained RL recipe coupling MO-GRPO with a multiplicative composite reward over LLM rubrics (text and reference-anchored audio), verifiable signals (IFEval, safety, latency, FDB timing), and audio-health gates (DNSMOS, silence/clipping/RMS).

🔬 Reward-Hacking Analysis

Single-family reward probes show no isolated reward suffices: each reaches high scalar reward while collapsing duplex-FDB to zero and leaving 26–49% of rollouts empty, whereas the composite cuts the empty rate to 4% and preserves duplex behavior.

📈 State-of-the-Art Open Source

SteerDuplex improves the strongest open-source duplex baselines by +22.8 points on SteerBench APR and +7.9 points on Audio MultiChallenge ARS while remaining competitive on Full-Duplex-Bench v1/v2 and VoiceBench.

Technical Approach

Backbone & Conditioning

SteerDuplex is a post-training recipe, not a new architecture. It keeps the Moshi-style full-duplex backbone (Défossez et al., 2024)—three jointly modeled streams (user audio, agent audio, agent text), hierarchical audio tokens, and a time-aligned Inner Monologue text channel at ~200ms latency. A natural-language steerability system prompt is prepended to the agent-text stream at the start of every dialogue; both audio streams stay silent during the prefix, and the same layout is used in SFT, RL, and inference to avoid train/deploy distribution shift.

MO-GRPO Optimization

Post-training uses on-policy, group-relative reinforcement learning. The default variant is Multi-Objective GRPO, which z-scores each reward component within the group and adds a 1/√K factor for a K-component reward so the magnitude of the combined advantage stays stable as components are added or removed—the only variant whose update magnitude remains stable across the reward sweep.

Composite Reward

The reward has three families: audio-aware rubric rewards scored by a frontier multimodal judge over text and delivery; verifiable rewards covering IFEval-style constraints, safety refusal, word-count and words-per-minute targets, response latency, and FDB-style timing; and audio-health rewards combining clipping, silence, RMS, minimum-duration gates, and DNSMOS. Aggregation is multiplicative, so a silent, mistimed, unsafe, or off-instruction rollout cannot be rescued by a high score on any single component. A reference-audio clip anchors each audio-steering reward, turning subjective style judgments ("cheerful", "formal") into paired comparisons against a fixed target.

Training Setup

  • SFT on 7,024.6 hours of curated synthetic and natural conversational speech (13.7-hour validation split), full fine-tuning on 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs with AdamW at learning rate 1×10⁻⁵.
  • RL on 2,000 curated multi-turn scenarios: full-parameter MO-GRPO at learning rate 2×10⁻⁷, group size G=16, 6 prompts per iteration, 40 iterations.
  • Adaptive KL control around target 0.10 (β₀=0.08), PPO clip 0.18, multiplicative reward aggregation by default.

Experimental Results

SteerDuplex pushes the open-source frontier substantially closer to proprietary systems on every benchmark targeting steerability, multi-turn robustness, and task-grounded duplex behavior—while preserving the streaming fidelity that motivates full-duplex modeling.

Key Findings

  • Steerability gains: +22.8 SteerBench APR over the strongest open baseline, with tone (+33.0), persona (+37.5), and style/accent (+28.2) seeing the largest lifts.
  • Multi-turn robustness: +7.9 Audio MultiChallenge ARS, and voice editing moves off the 0.00 floor that every prior open-source duplex model sits at.
  • Streaming preserved: KL-constrained small steps keep FDB v1 intact while FDB v2 climbs, so steering gains do not degrade interactive behavior.
  • No single reward suffices: isolated probes leave 26–49% of rollouts empty and collapse duplex-FDB to zero; the multiplicative composite cuts the empty rate to 4%.
  • Residual gap: the remaining 14–41-point gap to GPT-4o Realtime and Gemini Live reflects backbone scale, codec expressiveness, and judge supervision—pointing to joint backbone–codec scaling as a next step.

Limitations

SteerBench focuses on controlled English steering requests with fixed reference clips, and does not cover all languages, dialects, recording conditions, or long-horizon personalization. The audio-rubric judge depends on frontier multimodal evaluation, so absolute scores should be read alongside transcript validity, audio-health diagnostics, and downstream benchmarks. The model also inherits the latency, codec, and single-voice constraints of the Moshi-style backbone, bounding how far post-training alone can move the interaction frontier.
